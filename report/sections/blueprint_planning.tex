\section{Step 5: Blueprint Planning via a Three-Stage Stochastic Program}

OR scheduling decomposes into three planning levels~\cite{samudra2016}: the Case
Mix Problem (strategic), the Master Surgery Scheduling Problem (MSSP, tactical),
and the Surgery Scheduling Problem (SSP, operational). Step~3 addresses the SSP;
this step prepends a tactical MSSP first stage, yielding a three-stage
stochastic program whose first stage fixes a \emph{blueprint}: how many patients
of each type to schedule per session before the waiting list is known.

The scope is one week of three ORT sessions. As all sessions serve the same
specialty, the specialty variables $V_{hq}$ and mismatch penalty $D_{hq}$ of
Step~3 are dropped, and every session is always open:
\begin{equation}
  Z_h = 1 \qquad \forall\, h \in H.
  \label{eq:allopen}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Patient Subgroup Definition}
\label{sec:clustering}
The blueprint is fixed before the waiting list is revealed, so it cannot
reference individual patients; moreover the mix spans many surgery types that
each occur only sporadically. Patients are therefore aggregated into subgroups
$g \in G$ by $k$-means clustering of procedure types in the $(\mu, \sigma)$ space
of duration mean and standard deviation. This extends the label-based grouping
of~\cite{sheweita2021} by capturing scheduling risk ($\sigma$)
alongside expected OR consumption ($\mu$). Three clusters result, \textbf{S}
(short, low $\mu$/$\sigma$), \textbf{M} (medium), and \textbf{L} (long, high
$\mu$ and/or $\sigma$), giving $G = \{\mathrm{S}, \mathrm{M}, \mathrm{L}\}$.
Within a subgroup durations are approximately exchangeable, justifying the
subgroup count $N_{gh}$ as the Stage-1 decision.

\subsection{Three-Stage Structure and Scenario Tree}
\label{sec:three_stage_structure}
Uncertainty resolves in two phases. \textbf{Stage~1} (MSSP) fixes the blueprint
$N_{gh}$. \textbf{Stage~2} (SSP) reveals the waiting-list realisation $r \in R$
and assigns, sequences, and times patients ($X_{ijh}$, $Y_{ph}$, $A_p$, $Z_h$,
$U_{ph}$, indexed by $r$). \textbf{Stage~3} observes surgery durations and
evaluates the schedule ($S_{ps}$, $W_{ps}$, $I_{pp's}$, $O_{hs}$, indexed by
$rs$). A node-oriented formulation indexes variables by the nodes of a scenario
tree with root $n_0$, waiting-list nodes $r$, and leaves $rs$; non-anticipativity
is then structural, $N_{gh}$ lives at $n_0$, Stage-2 variables at $r$, Stage-3
variables at $rs$, so no explicit non-anticipativity constraints are needed.

\subsection{Extended Model}
\label{sec:extended_model}
The set $r \in R$ indexes waiting-list realisations as $s \in S$ indexes duration
scenarios, giving $|R| \times |S|$ leaves with probabilities $\pi_r$ and
$\pi_{s|r}$. Each $r$ defines a patient set $P_r$ with subgroup partition
$P_{gr}$. The new Stage-1 variable $N_{gh} \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}$ is the number
of subgroup-$g$ patients allowed in session~$h$.

\paragraph{Objective function.}
Without a cost on $N_{gh}$ the optimiser would set every quota to
$\max_r |P_{gr}|$ and impose no structure, so a per-slot penalty $\beta_5$ is
added. This trades blueprint specificity against operational flexibility: a
tighter blueprint reduces planning ambiguity but may over-constrain Stage~2. We
set $\beta_5 = 1$; a sensitivity analysis is left for future work.
\begin{align}
  \min \; & \beta_5 \sum_{h \in H} \sum_{g \in G} N_{gh}
    + \sum_{r \in R} \pi_r \sum_{s \in S} \pi_{s|r} \left(
        \beta_1 \sum_{p} W_{prs} \right. \nonumber \\
  & \left. \quad
      + \; \beta_2 \sum_{\substack{p,p' \in P_r\\p \neq p'}} I_{pp'rs}
      + \beta_3 \sum_{h} O_{hrs}
    \right) \label{eq:three_stage_obj}
\end{align}

\paragraph{Blueprint linking constraint.}
The blueprint caps the number of patients of each subgroup per session:
\begin{equation}
  \sum_{p \in P_{gr}} Y_{phr} \leq N_{gh}
  \qquad \forall\, g \in G,\; h \in H,\; r \in R
  \label{eq:blueprint_link}
\end{equation}
The inequality allows under-utilisation: it is slack when realisation~$r$ holds
fewer subgroup-$g$ patients than the quota and binding otherwise. All remaining
Step~3 constraints (excluding the specialty
constraints~\eqref{eq:spec} and~\eqref{eq:viol}) carry forward as Stage-2
($r$-indexed) and Stage-3 ($rs$-indexed) constraints. The deterministic
equivalent scales as $\mathcal{O}(|R| \cdot |P|^2 \cdot |H|)$ binary and
$\mathcal{O}(|R| \cdot |S| \cdot (|P|^2 + |H|))$ continuous variables;
tractability is kept by SAA with moderate $|R|$ and $|S|$.

\paragraph{Symmetry breaking.}
As the three sessions are identical, the $|H|!$ index symmetry is removed by
ordering the columns on the lexicographic capacity key
$100\,N_{\mathrm{L}h} + 10\,N_{\mathrm{M}h} + N_{\mathrm{S}h}$ (non-increasing
in~$h$), fixing a canonical labelling (session~0 heaviest) without affecting
optimality.

\subsection{Instance Design}
\label{sec:bp_instance}
Surgery types are clustered on the full ORT history
(\texttt{ort\_patient\_data.csv}); the subgroups are summarised in
Table~\ref{tab:bp_clusters}, with the mix dominated by short procedures (50 of
68 types) and three long, high-variance types forming~L. Each waiting-list
realisation pools the patients of three documented ORT sessions drawn at random
from \texttt{step\_5\_sample\_sessions.csv}; we use $|R| = 3$ equiprobable
realisations and $|S| = 5$ duration scenarios each (importance sampling,
$k = 1$), with $\beta_5 = 1$. The three realisations contain $16$, $14$, and

\subsection{Blueprint Results and Managerial Insights}
\label{sec:bp_results}
Table~\ref{tab:bp_quota} reports the blueprint. The per-subgroup totals
(15~S, 2~M, 1~L) equal the largest subgroup count observed across the three
realisations: with $\beta_5 = 1$ the model provisions exactly enough capacity to
absorb the worst-case realisation and no more, and this part of the solution is
provably tight (the solver lower bound equals the slot cost). The single long
case is isolated in one session and the medium cases concentrated in another,
leaving the rest for short, high-throughput procedures.

Managerially, a robust weekly blueprint should reserve one session for the long,
high-variance procedures and devote the others to a high volume of short cases,
rather than spreading long cases evenly. Isolating the variance-driving cases
limits how far a single overrun cascades into downstream waiting and overtime,
while the short-case sessions stay dense and predictable.

\paragraph{Tractability.}
The Big-$M$ sequencing constraints, compounded by three nested Stage-2 problems,
leave a sizeable residual gap on the \emph{operational} cost within the
600-second limit, while the capacities $N_{gh}$ reach the proven lower bound.

\subsection{Blueprint Stability Across Sampling Seeds}
\label{sec:bp_consistency}
A single solve reflects one SAA draw. To check that the quota table is a
property of the data rather than an artefact of one sample, the program is
re-solved over nine seeds $\theta \in \{42, \ldots, 50\}$ with a larger instance
($|R|=5$, $|S|=20$, LHS), holding the clustering fixed so the S/M/L labels stay
comparable (Appendix~\ref{app:bp_stability}). Three regularities emerge
(Tables~\ref{tab:bp_seeds_quality}--\ref{tab:bp_seeds_quotas}). The \emph{S
total is the most stable dimension} (modal $\sum_h N_{Sh} = 16$, matching the
largest short-case count and confirming worst-case provisioning without slack).
The \emph{L allocation is structurally invariant}: every seed that provisions a
long slot concentrates it in session~0, an emergent outcome rather than an
artefact of the symmetry cut. The \emph{M subgroup is the main source of
variability} ($\sum_h N_{Mh} \in [1,4]$). This supports three planning rules:
(i)~designate one session as the long-case anchor; (ii)~provision at least one
long slot by default, since long types appear in $\approx$55\% of sampled
waiting lists despite being three of 68 types; and (iii)~distribute medium slots
uniformly, as medium cases ($\approx$126\,min) do not materially raise overtime
risk.

\subsection{Recommended Blueprint}
\label{sec:bp_robust}
Aggregating the stability evidence, solution quality, and the rules above,
seed~42 is adopted as the reference policy: it attains the lowest in-sample
objective (95.7) and the second-lowest optimality gap (80.2\%), and its quotas
match the modal totals ($\sum_h N_{Sh} = 16$, $\sum_h N_{Mh} = 3$,
$\sum_h N_{Lh} = 1$). The session-level allocation---$S{:}[6,5,5]$,
$M{:}[1,1,1]$, $L{:}[1,0,0]$---spreads short and medium capacity symmetrically
while isolating the long case in session~0. This blueprint is used in the
operational comparison below.

\subsection{Operational Cost of Imposing the Blueprint}
\label{sec:bp_apply}
The blueprint is fixed before the waiting list is known; to price obedience to
it, a comparison instance is built as a Stage-2 realisation---three documented
ORT sessions drawn at random and pooled---but retaining the recorded assignment
and sequence so the operational Step~1--3 chain applies unchanged, with types
mapped to $G$ by the same clustering. The chain is solved twice: a \emph{baseline}
run with free assignment, and a \emph{restricted} run adding
$\sum_{p \in P_g} Y_{ph} \leq N_{gh}$. Both policies are evaluated out of sample
on one shared scenario set (as in the VSS analysis), so their cost difference is
the \emph{operational cost of the blueprint}, non-negative up to the solves'
optimality gaps since the restricted region is a subset of the baseline.

We repeat the comparison over six seeds. The blueprint binds only at the
full-assignment step: in Steps~1 and~2 the assignment is fixed from the
documented schedule, so baseline and blueprint coincide exactly for every seed
(Appendix~\ref{app:bp_comparison}). Figure~\ref{fig:bp_comparison_step3} shows
the Step~3 comparison. Where both solves are feasible, the out-of-sample costs
differ only marginally and with mixed sign (e.g.\ $41.2 \to 40.6$,
$75.5 \to 72.6$, $66.6 \to 67.1$), i.e.\ within the large residual optimality
gaps: imposing the tactical template is \emph{operationally close to free}. The
exception is seed~54, where the restricted problem is \emph{infeasible}: the
drawn waiting list holds 17 short cases but the blueprint provisions only 16
S-slots, so the worst-case-tight quota cannot absorb a heavier-than-sampled
realisation. This exposes the central trade-off of tight provisioning---a
negligible flexibility cost when feasible, but no headroom above the sampled
worst case---and motivates a small safety margin on the dominant subgroup.
