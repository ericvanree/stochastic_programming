\section{Step 5: Blueprint Planning via a Three-Stage Stochastic Program}

OR scheduling decomposes into three planning levels~\cite{samudra2016}: the Case
Mix Problem (strategic), the Master Surgery Scheduling Problem (MSSP, tactical),
and the Surgery Scheduling Problem (SSP, operational). Step~3 addresses the SSP;
this step prepends a tactical MSSP first stage, yielding a three-stage
stochastic program whose first stage fixes a \emph{blueprint}: how many patients
of each type to schedule per session before the waiting list is known.

The scope is one week of three ORT sessions. As all sessions serve the same
specialty, the specialty variables $V_{hq}$ and mismatch penalty $D_{hq}$ of
Step~3 are dropped, and every session is always open:
\begin{equation}
  Z_h = 1 \qquad \forall\, h \in H.
  \label{eq:allopen}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Patient Subgroup Definition}
\label{sec:clustering}
The blueprint is fixed before the waiting list is revealed, so it cannot
reference individual patients; moreover the mix spans many surgery types that
each occur only sporadically. Patients are therefore aggregated into subgroups
$g \in G$ by $k$-means clustering of procedure types in the $(\mu, \sigma)$ space
of duration mean and standard deviation. This extends the label-based grouping
of~\cite{sheweita2021} by capturing scheduling risk ($\sigma$)
alongside expected OR consumption ($\mu$). Three clusters result, \textbf{S}
(short, low $\mu$/$\sigma$), \textbf{M} (medium), and \textbf{L} (long, high
$\mu$ and/or $\sigma$), giving $G = \{\mathrm{S}, \mathrm{M}, \mathrm{L}\}$.
Within a subgroup durations are approximately exchangeable, justifying the
subgroup count $N_{gh}$ as the Stage-1 decision.

\subsection{Three-Stage Structure and Scenario Tree}
\label{sec:three_stage_structure}
Uncertainty resolves in two phases. \textbf{Stage~1} (MSSP) fixes the blueprint
$N_{gh}$. \textbf{Stage~2} (SSP) reveals the waiting-list realisation $r \in R$
and assigns, sequences, and times patients ($X_{ijh}$, $Y_{ph}$, $A_p$, $Z_h$,
$U_{ph}$, indexed by $r$). \textbf{Stage~3} observes surgery durations and
evaluates the schedule ($S_{ps}$, $W_{ps}$, $I_{pp's}$, $O_{hs}$, indexed by
$rs$). A node-oriented formulation indexes variables by the nodes of a scenario
tree with root $n_0$, waiting-list nodes $r$, and leaves $rs$; non-anticipativity
is then structural, $N_{gh}$ is set at $n_0$, Stage-2 variables at $r$, Stage-3
variables at $rs$, so no explicit non-anticipativity constraints are needed.

\subsection{Extended Model}
\label{sec:extended_model}
The set $r \in R$ indexes waiting-list realisations as $s \in S$ indexes duration
scenarios, giving $|R| \times |S|$ leaves with probabilities $\pi_r$ and
$\pi_{s|r}$. Each $r$ defines a patient set $P_r$ with subgroup partition
$P_{gr}$. The new Stage-1 variable $N_{gh} \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}$ is the number
of subgroup-$g$ patients allowed in session~$h$.

\paragraph{Objective function.}
Without a cost on $N_{gh}$ the optimiser would set every quota to
$\max_r |P_{gr}|$ and impose no structure, so a per-slot penalty $\beta_5$ is
added. This trades blueprint specificity against operational flexibility: a
tighter blueprint reduces planning ambiguity but may over-constrain Stage~2. We
set $\beta_5 = 1$. 
\begin{align}
  \min \; & \beta_5 \sum_{h \in H} \sum_{g \in G} N_{gh}
    + \sum_{r \in R} \pi_r \sum_{s \in S} \pi_{s|r} \left(
        \beta_1 \sum_{p} W_{prs} \right. \nonumber \\
  & \left. \quad
      + \; \beta_2 \sum_{\substack{p,p' \in P_r\\p \neq p'}} I_{pp'rs}
      + \beta_3 \sum_{h} O_{hrs}
    \right) \label{eq:three_stage_obj}
\end{align}

\paragraph{Blueprint linking constraint.}
The blueprint caps the number of patients of each subgroup per session:
\begin{equation}
  \sum_{p \in P_{gr}} Y_{phr} \leq N_{gh}
  \qquad \forall\, g \in G,\; h \in H,\; r \in R
  \label{eq:blueprint_link}
\end{equation}
The inequality allows under-utilisation: it is slack when realisation~$r$ holds
fewer subgroup-$g$ patients than the quota and binding otherwise. All remaining
Step~3 constraints (excluding the specialty
constraints~\eqref{eq:spec} and~\eqref{eq:viol}) carry forward as Stage-2
($r$-indexed) and Stage-3 ($rs$-indexed) constraints. The deterministic
equivalent scales as $\mathcal{O}(|R| \cdot |P|^2 \cdot |H|)$ binary and
$\mathcal{O}(|R| \cdot |S| \cdot (|P|^2 + |H|))$ continuous variables;
tractability is kept by SAA with moderate $|R|$ and $|S|$.

\paragraph{Symmetry breaking.}
As the three sessions are identical, the $|H|!$ index symmetry is removed by
ordering the columns on the lexicographic capacity key
$100\,N_{\mathrm{L}h} + 10\,N_{\mathrm{M}h} + N_{\mathrm{S}h}$ (non-increasing
in~$h$), without affecting
optimality.

\subsection{Blueprint Results and Managerial Insights}
\label{sec:bp_results}

A single solve reflects one SAA draw. To check that the resulting raster from the instance design is a
property of the data rather than an artefact of one sample, blueprints were generated on 9 different seeds, and the per-seed blueprint solutions $N_{gh}$ are listed by session in Appendix~\ref{app:bp_stability} (Table~\ref{tab:bp_seeds_sessions}).

Based on the different blueprints that were derived, we find that most blueprints represent similar rasters in which the patient types are evenly spread over the sessions.
This seems to suggest that a good appointment heuristic divides treatments with similar characteristics rather evenly over the different sessions.
We identify three planning rules to which the blueprints seem to adhere:
(i)~designate one session for the long-cases; (ii)~provision at least one
long slot by default, since long types appear in $\approx$55\% of sampled
waiting lists despite being only three of 68 types; and (iii)~distribute short and medium slots
uniformly.

The blueprint generated with $s=42$ represents the different rasters quite well, and is provided in Table~\ref{tab:bp_quota}. The single long
case is isolated in one session and the short and medium cases are evenly distributed over the sessions.
Seed~42 is therefore adopted as a reference raster. In addition, it attains the lowest in-sample
objective (95.7), and the second-lowest optimality gap (80.2\%), supporting this decision. 
The quality of this blueprint will be evaluated in the operational comparison below.

\subsection{Operational Cost of Imposing the Blueprint}
\label{sec:bp_apply}
To assess the effect of raster implementations on solution quality, the raster derived with $s=42$ is used to evaluate the impact of the raster on the solution quality of a certain patient set realization. 

The blueprint is fixed before the waiting list is known. To evaluate the impact of implementing such blueprint, a comparison instance is built by randomly drawing three documented
ORT sessions as a potential realization of the waiting list for the three sessions. The realization is solved twice: once for a baseline
run with free assignment, and a restricted run adding
$\sum_{p \in P_g} Y_{ph} \leq N_{gh}$ to adhere to the blueprint. This way, both policies are evaluated
on one shared scenario. 

We repeat the comparison over six seeds. Figure~\ref{fig:bp_comparison_step3} shows
the Step~3 comparison. Where both solves are feasible, the out-of-sample costs
differ only marginally and with mixed sign (e.g.\ $41.2 \to 40.6$,
$75.5 \to 72.6$, $66.6 \to 67.1$), i.e.\ within the large residual optimality
gaps: imposing the tactical template is operationally close to free. The
exception is seed~54, where the restricted problem is infeasible: the
drawn waiting list holds 17 short cases but the blueprint provisions only 16
S-slots, so not all patients in that category can be scheduled under the blueprint. 
This exposes one of the central trade-offs of tight blueprints; a
negligible flexibility cost when feasible, but no guarantee that a feasible schedule can be found for
all patients on the waiting list in all scenarios.
