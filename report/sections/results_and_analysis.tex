For every waiting list the out-of-sample cost exceeds the in-sample cost. This gap is a direct consequence of overfitting: the first-stage schedule is optimised against the 20 training scenarios, so it fits the particular realisations seen during training more tightly than the independent evaluation scenarios.

Focusing on the timing-related penalties, waiting is consistently the largest contributor, followed by idle time and then overtime. This ordering is stable across all four waiting lists and across both the in-sample and out-of-sample bars.

A stronger effect is visible for two of the four waiting lists (wl7 and wl13), where the specialty-mismatch penalty (D) dominates the objective. In these instances patients are assigned to a session whose specialisation differs from the patient's own specialisation. Such a mismatch incurs a large penalty, since it should be avoided whenever a matching session is available; the model only resorts to it when the available session capacity for the patient's own specialisation is insufficient to accommodate the waiting list.

\subsection{Expected Value of Perfect Information}

The Expected Value of Perfect Information (EVPI) quantifies the expected gain from knowing
the exact scenario before making any decisions. It is estimated as follows.

First, the stochastic MILP is solved using $S$ training scenarios drawn from the
chosen sampling distribution, yielding a here-and-now policy
$(X^*, Y^*, A^*, D^*)$.
Second, $I$ independent evaluation scenarios are generated.
For each evaluation scenario $i$, two objective values are computed:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $z_{\text{policy}}(i)$: the cost using the policy trained on S evaluated on scenario $i$.
    \item $z_{\text{perf}}(i)$: the cost with perfect information, obtained by solving the MILP with scenario $i$ as the sole scenario, so all first-stage decisions can be optimised with full knowledge of the realised durations.
\end{itemize}
The value of perfect information for scenario $i$ is
\[
    \text{VPI}(i) = z_{\text{policy}}(i) - z_{\text{perf}}(i) \;\geq\; 0,
\]
and the EVPI is estimated as the sample mean over the $I$ evaluation scenarios:
\[
    \widehat{\text{EVPI}} = \frac{1}{I} \sum_{i=1}^{I} \text{VPI}(i).
\]

Using the policy trained on $S = 20$ scenarios, Table~\ref{tab:vpi} presents
the per-scenario costs over $I = 10$ independent evaluation scenarios. In 7 out
of 10 evaluation scenarios, $z_{\text{perf}}(i) = 0$, meaning that a
penalty-free schedule exists when the surgery durations are known in advance.
For these scenarios, the entire cost incurred by the policy is attributable to
uncertainty, and $\text{VPI}(i) = z_{\text{policy}}(i)$. Only 3 scenarios
($i = 1$, $i = 4$ and $i = 8$) still require overtime under perfect
information, suggesting that these particular realisations of surgery durations
are inherently expensive to schedule regardless of the decision rule applied;
even for these, the residual cost is small ($z_{\text{perf}}(4) = 0.065$)
except where the realised durations are genuinely demanding. The estimated
expected value of perfect information over the 10 evaluation scenarios is
$\widehat{\text{EVPI}} = 117.91$ (standard deviation $34.63$, $95\%$ confidence
interval $[96.45, 139.37]$), indicating that on average a large share of the
policy cost could in principle be eliminated by resolving duration uncertainty
prior to scheduling.

\subsection{Value of the Stochastic Solution}
\label{subsec:VSS}

The Value of the Stochastic Solution (VSS) measures the benefit of explicitly
accounting for duration uncertainty rather than planning against a single
deterministic forecast. It is defined as
\[
    \text{VSS} = \text{EEV} - \text{RP},
\]
where RP (the \emph{recourse problem}) is the expected out-of-sample cost of the
stochastic (SAA) policy, and EEV (the \emph{expected result of using the
expected value solution}) is the expected out-of-sample cost of the
deterministic policy.

The estimation procedure consists of three steps.
First, the \emph{expected value} (EV) policy is obtained by solving the MILP with
a single scenario in which every surgery takes its expected duration; this yields
a here-and-now schedule $(X^{\text{EV}}, Y^{\text{EV}}, A^{\text{EV}}, D^{\text{EV}})$.
Second, the stochastic (SAA) policy is obtained by solving the MILP with $N$
training scenarios drawn from the chosen sampling distribution, yielding
$(X^{\text{SAA}}, Y^{\text{SAA}}, A^{\text{SAA}}, D^{\text{SAA}})$.
Third, both fixed first-stage policies are evaluated on the \emph{same}
independent out-of-sample set of $N'$ scenarios, generated with a seed distinct
from those used to train either policy. Sharing a single evaluation set
eliminates evaluation-sample noise from the difference, so that
$\text{VSS} \geq 0$ holds up to solver optimality gaps rather than being
polluted by two independent sample means. The two out-of-sample means are
\[
    \text{EEV} = \frac{1}{N'} \sum_{i=1}^{N'} z\!\left(\text{EV policy}, i\right),
    \qquad
    \text{RP} = \frac{1}{N'} \sum_{i=1}^{N'} z\!\left(\text{SAA policy}, i\right),
\]
where $z(\cdot, i)$ is the realised cost of a fixed first-stage policy under
evaluation scenario $i$, computed by the recourse simulation. A large VSS
indicates that incorporating uncertainty into the planning model yields a
substantial cost reduction over the naive deterministic plan.

We estimate the VSS for the same four waiting lists used in
Section~\ref{subsec:traineval}, again using $N = 20$ training scenarios sampled
with LHS. The out-of-sample EEV and RP for the full MILP (Step 3) are shown in
Figure~\ref{fig:vss_step3}; the corresponding results for Step 1 (timing only)
and Step 2 (sequencing and timing) are reported in
Appendix~\ref{app:vss_breakdowns}.

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1\linewidth]{Figures/vss_eev_rp_step3.png}
    \caption{Out-of-sample deterministic policy and stochastic policy for the full MILP on four waiting lists. The annotated gap is the estimated VSS, both in absolute terms and as a percentage of the EEV.}
    \label{fig:vss_step3}
\end{figure}

For Steps 1 and 2 the estimated VSS is positive for all four waiting lists,
ranging from $13\%$ to $34\%$ of the deterministic out-of-sample cost. This is
the expected outcome: the EV policy is optimised against a single scenario in
which every surgery takes its mean duration and therefore ignores the
variability of the realised durations, so it is systematically outperformed by
the SAA policy on the out-of-sample set.

For the full MILP (Step 3) this pattern no longer holds uniformly. The VSS
remains clearly positive for two waiting lists (wl4 and wl10, at $+34\%$ and
$+28\%$ respectively), but turns negative for the other two (wl7 at $-13\%$ and
wl13 at $-4\%$). As discussed, a negative VSS is highly unlikely to happen show, because the stochastic model models the variation in the sample set better than the deterministic model.
The most plausible explanation is computational. In Step 3 neither the
deterministic nor the stochastic instance is solved to proven optimality within
the imposed time limit of $300$\,s. The deterministic problem contains a single
scenario, whereas the stochastic problem replicates the second-stage structure
across all $20$ training scenarios and is consequently far larger and harder to
solve. Within the same wall-clock budget the deterministic run attains a smaller
optimality gap than the stochastic run, so the EV policy it returns can happen
to evaluate better out-of-sample than the prematurely terminated SAA policy.
This is precisely the situation that produces a spurious negative VSS, and it is
consistent with the two affected waiting lists (wl7 and wl13) being exactly
those whose objectives are dominated by the specialty-mismatch term identified
in Section~\ref{subsec:traineval}, which makes their MILPs the most demanding to
solve. We therefore expect that, if the Step 3 training instances were solved to
optimality as they effectively are in Steps 1 and 2, the VSS would be
non-negative for all four waiting lists.
