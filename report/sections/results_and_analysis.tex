\subsection{Training and evaluation resutls}
\label{subsec:traineval}
With the results found in Section~\ref{subsec:SAAresults}, we select the number of training scenarios to be 20 and lhs as sampling technique to compare the training solution values and evaluation values for 4 different waiting lists. The comparison for the full MILP (Step 3) is depicted in Figure~\ref{fig:objective_breakdown_step3}; the corresponding breakdowns for Step 1 (timing only) and Step 2 (sequencing and timing) are reported in Appendix~\ref{app:objective_breakdowns}.

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.72\linewidth]{Figures/objective_breakdown_step3.png}
    \caption{Objective breakdown for the full MILP (Step 3) on four waiting lists}
    \label{fig:objective_breakdown_step3}
\end{figure}

For every waiting list the out-of-sample cost exceeds the in-sample cost. This gap is a direct consequence of overfitting: the first-stage schedule is optimised against the 20 training scenarios, so it fits the particular realisations seen during training more tightly than the independent evaluation scenarios.

Focusing on the timing-related penalties, waiting is consistently the largest contributor on the out-of-sample objective, followed by idle time and then overtime. This ordering is stable across all four waiting lists and across both the in-sample and out-of-sample bars.

For wl7 and wl13 the specialty-mismatch penalty (D) dominates the objective. In these instances patients are assigned to a session whose specialisation differs from the patient's own specialisation. Such a mismatch incurs a large penalty, since it should be avoided whenever a matching session is available; the model only resorts to it when the available session capacity for the patient's own specialisation is insufficient to accommodate the waiting list.

\subsection{Expected Value of Perfect Information}
\label{subsec:EVPI}

The Expected Value of Perfect Information (EVPI) is the expected gain from
knowing the realised scenario before deciding. We solve the stochastic MILP on
$S$ training scenarios to obtain a here-and-now policy, generate $I$ independent
evaluation scenarios, and for each compute the policy cost $z_{\text{policy}}(i)$
and the perfect-information cost $z_{\text{perf}}(i)$ (the MILP solved with
scenario $i$ as its sole scenario, so all first-stage decisions use full
knowledge of the realised durations). The per-scenario value is
$\text{VPI}(i) = z_{\text{policy}}(i) - z_{\text{perf}}(i) \geq 0$, and the EVPI
is its sample mean,
\[
    \widehat{\text{EVPI}} = \frac{1}{I} \sum_{i=1}^{I} \text{VPI}(i).
\]

Using the policy trained on $S = 20$ scenarios, Table~\ref{tab:vpi} presents
the per-scenario costs over $I = 10$ independent evaluation scenarios from wl4. In 7 out
of 10 evaluation scenarios, $z_{\text{perf}}(i) = 0$, meaning that a
penalty-free schedule exists when the surgery durations are known in advance.
For these scenarios, the entire cost incurred by the policy is attributable to
uncertainty, and $\text{VPI}(i) = z_{\text{policy}}(i)$. Only 3 scenarios
($i = 1$, $i = 4$ and $i = 8$) still require overtime under perfect
information, suggesting that these particular realisations of surgery durations
are inherently expensive to schedule regardless of the decision rule applied.
The estimated expected value of perfect information over the 10 evaluation scenarios is
$\widehat{\text{EVPI}} = 117.91$ (standard deviation $34.63$, $95\%$ confidence
interval $[96.45, 139.37]$), indicating that on average a large share of the
policy cost could in principle be eliminated by resolving duration uncertainty
prior to scheduling.

\subsection{Value of the Stochastic Solution}
\label{subsec:VSS}

The Value of the Stochastic Solution (VSS) measures the benefit of explicitly
accounting for duration uncertainty rather than planning against a single
deterministic forecast. It is defined as
\[
    \text{VSS} = \text{EEV} - \text{RP},
\]
where RP (the \emph{recourse problem}) is the expected out-of-sample cost of the
stochastic (SAA) policy, and EEV (the \emph{expected result of using the
expected value solution}) is the expected out-of-sample cost of the
deterministic policy.

We obtain the \emph{expected value} (EV) policy by solving the MILP with a single
mean-duration scenario, and the stochastic (SAA) policy by solving it with $N$
training scenarios. Both fixed first-stage policies are then evaluated on the
\emph{same} independent out-of-sample set of $N'$ scenarios (a seed distinct from
either training set), giving
\[
    \text{EEV} = \frac{1}{N'} \sum_{i=1}^{N'} z\!\left(\text{EV policy}, i\right),
    \qquad
    \text{RP} = \frac{1}{N'} \sum_{i=1}^{N'} z\!\left(\text{SAA policy}, i\right),
\]
where $z(\cdot, i)$ is the recourse-simulated cost of a fixed policy under
scenario $i$. Sharing one evaluation set removes evaluation-sample noise, so
$\text{VSS} \geq 0$ holds up to solver optimality gaps; a large VSS means
modelling uncertainty yields a substantial gain over the deterministic plan.

We estimate the VSS for the same four waiting lists used in
Section~\ref{subsec:traineval}, again using $N = 20$ training scenarios sampled
with LHS. The out-of-sample EEV and RP for the full MILP (Step 3) are shown in
Figure~\ref{fig:vss_step3}; the corresponding results for Step 1 (timing only)
and Step 2 (sequencing and timing) are reported in
Appendix~\ref{app:vss_breakdowns}.

\begin{figure}[t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.72\linewidth]{Figures/vss_eev_rp_step3.png}
    \caption{Out-of-sample deterministic policy and stochastic policy for the full MILP on four waiting lists. The annotated gap is the estimated VSS, both in absolute terms and as a percentage of the EEV.}
    \label{fig:vss_step3}
\end{figure}

For Steps 1 and 2 the estimated VSS is positive for all four waiting lists,
ranging from $13\%$ to $34\%$ of the deterministic out-of-sample cost. This is
the expected outcome: the EV policy is optimised against a single scenario in
which every surgery takes its mean duration and therefore ignores the
variability of the realised durations, so it is systematically outperformed by
the SAA policy on the out-of-sample set.

For the full MILP (Step 3) this pattern breaks: the VSS stays clearly positive
for wl4 and wl10 ($+34\%$, $+28\%$) but turns negative for wl7 ($-13\%$) and wl13
($-4\%$). A genuinely negative VSS is highly unlikely, so the most plausible
explanation is computational. Within the $300$\,s limit neither instance is
solved to optimality, but the single-scenario deterministic problem attains a
smaller gap than the far larger stochastic one, so its EV policy can happen to
evaluate better out of sample than the prematurely terminated SAA policy. This
spurious effect is consistent with the two affected lists being exactly those
dominated by the specialty-mismatch term (Section~\ref{subsec:traineval}), whose
MILPs are hardest to solve; solved to optimality, as in Steps 1 and 2, the VSS
would be non-negative throughout.

\subsection{Managerial Insights on Operational Scheduling}
\label{subsec:operational_insights}
The Step~1--4 results translate into several insights for day-to-day OR
scheduling. First, waiting time is consistently the dominant cost component
(Section~\ref{subsec:traineval}); operationally, slack should be built in early
in each session, as the optimiser is willing to accept some idle time to stop a
single overrun from cascading into the waiting time of every downstream patient.
Second, the specialty-mismatch penalty is incurred only when own-specialty
capacity is insufficient: a forced mismatch is therefore a capacity-shortfall
signal rather than a free scheduling choice, and blocks should be kept
specialty-pure whenever a matching session is available. Third, the value of the
stochastic solution is positive wherever the model converges and largest when
the patient-to-session assignment is free
(Section~\ref{subsec:VSS}); modelling duration uncertainty pays off most at the
assignment level, and planning against mean durations systematically
underperforms out of sample. Fourth, the large EVPI ($\approx$118,
Section~\ref{subsec:EVPI}) indicates that most cost stems from duration
uncertainty itself, so investing in better duration prediction or shorter
scheduling lead times has high operational value. Finally, the persistent
out-of-sample penalty relative to in-sample cost warns against over-tuning a
schedule to a handful of scenarios; the chosen $N \approx 20$ LHS scenarios
balance robustness against computational cost. These operational patterns are
revisited alongside the tactical blueprint findings in the conclusion.
